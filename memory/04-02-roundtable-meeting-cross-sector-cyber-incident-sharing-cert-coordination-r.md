## Summary
Banks, regulators, and national cybersecurity bodies agreed to activate a unified cross-sector information-sharing and incident-response ecosystem anchored by Nigeria's National CERT, coordinated by [[NGCAC]]/[[NGSAT]], and aligned with [[CBN]] supervisory requirements. The consensus is that anonymized sharing must replace competitive secrecy, with the Cybercrime Act 2015's 7-day reporting window treated as a floor — not the operational target. The national platform is operational but requires broader participation and disciplined governance.

## Key Points
- Near-real-time alerts required; [[CBN]] 2–3 hour reporting window is the operational target (Cybercrime Act 2015 mandates 7 days — too slow)
- Reinstate anonymized "bank fraud forum" model under [[NGCAC]]/[[NGSAT]] coordination extended across sectors
- Cultural distrust and reputational fear are primary barriers to sharing; anonymized-yet-actionable sharing required
- Pre-go-live security assessments must be mandated for all critical applications including cloud deployments
- Insider threats are a significant loss driver; continuous vetting, access reviews, and segregation of duties required
- SIM-based scams, mule accounts, and cross-channel fraud require joint protocols with telecoms and law enforcement
- 72-hour freeze release law hinders fund recovery — MOUs or legal reform needed
- Impending banking-exchange "handshake" for digital assets will compress response windows
- Under-reporting is the norm; reporting is not punitive, but non-reporting is sanctionable
- AI-assisted coding risks flagged as a vulnerability in developer training
