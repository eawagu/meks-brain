## Overview

The Nigerian courts' framework for awarding damages in breach-of-contract actions rests on three anchor rules: contract formation essentials, the reasonable-contemplation measure of damages (*Hadley v Baxendale*), and the unavailability of general damages in pure contract cases.

## Three Essentials of a Valid Contract

Offer, unqualified acceptance of that offer, and consideration. Referenced in *Tsokwa Motors Ltd v UBN Ltd* (1996) 9 NWLR (Pt 471) 129; *PTI v Uwamu* (2001) 5 NWLR (Pt 705) 112; *Omega Bank (Nig) Plc v OBC Ltd* (2005) LPELR-2636 (SC) per Edozie JSC; and (text-book authority) Cheshire and Fifoot, *Law of Contract* 9th Edition pp. 27, 31.

Burden on claimant in civil actions is **balance of probabilities** (*Akande v Adisa* (2012) 15 NWLR (Pt 1324) 538).

## Measure of Damages — the Hadley v Baxendale Rule

Per *Hadley v Baxendale* (1854) 9 Exch. 341 at 354, adopted in Nigerian jurisprudence: the aggrieved party recovers only such part of the loss actually resulting as was, **at the time of the contract, reasonably foreseeable as liable to result from the breach**. Foreseeability is measured by the knowledge possessed by the parties at contract formation — in particular by the party who later commits breach.

**Governing purpose:** put the party whose rights have been violated in the same position, so far as money can do, as if the rights had been observed.

Nigerian applications:

- *Swiss-Nigeria Wood Industries Ltd v Bogo* (1970) 6 NSC 235 — loss flowing naturally from breach
- *Jamma Engineering v Wrought Iron* (1970) NCLR 295 — direct consequence of breach
- *UBA v Folarin* (2002) LPELR-7178 (CA) — governing purpose of damages

## No General Damages in Pure Contract Claims

General damages are not recoverable in breach-of-contract actions. Recovery is limited to:

1. **Special damages** — actual foreseeable loss flowing from the breach, specifically pleaded and proved; and
2. **Restitution** — return of sums paid for consideration not received (e.g., a mobilization fee paid for work not done).

Claims for "general damages" in contract matters are routinely refused as conceptually misplaced.

## Operational Implication

When drafting demand letters or statements of claim for breach-of-contract disputes under Nigerian law:

- **Must plead special damages with particulars** — line-by-line attribution of each loss to the breach, with evidence of foreseeability at contract formation.
- **Must not include general damages** as a standalone head — the claim will fail.
- **Downstream business losses** (lost revenue, wasted staff costs, lost opportunities) recover only if evidence shows the breaching party knew or ought to have known at contract time that such losses would flow from the breach — Claimants who underplead foreseeability at formation surrender the larger commercial recovery even when the underlying loss is real.
