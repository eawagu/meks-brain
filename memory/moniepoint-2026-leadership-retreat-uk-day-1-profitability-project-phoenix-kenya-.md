## Summary

Day 1 retreat session on profitability discipline, multi-country platformization via [[Project Phoenix]], [[Kenya Launch]] via [[Sumac Microfinance Bank]] acquisition, and competitive/regulatory strategy. [[Tosin Eniolorunda]] set the 2026 PAT gates, defined Project Phoenix as "open-heart surgery" requiring complete product rebuild for multi-country scale, and framed Nigeria (MFB) becoming a business entity within a platform-centric structure.

## Key Points — Profitability

- **PAT Targets**: 20% run-rate by end-2026; 30% run-rate by Q2 2026 (30% depends on lending expansion).
- **2025 trajectory**: Q3 approached profitability, Q4 reversed due to scheme pricing shocks (notably [[Verve]]) and other disruptions.
- **Root cause**: lack of fine cost control across COGS/expenses + absent goal systems. Remedy: management primitives + stronger goal systems.
- **Opex focus**: depreciation, salaries, cloud costs (10% rise last month). Biggest lever: contribution margin via reduced promotions, commissions, processing costs.
- **Nigeria revenue outlook**: $680M this year. Cited as 6th in banking revenue among profitable Nigerian banks.
- **Revenue gap**: ~$1.5B theoretical benchmark vs ~$500M actual — attributed to lower LTVs, but leadership prioritizes impact in underserved markets.
- **Deposits are the only line above budget** — the key contribution margin driver; customers rarely switch primary accounts, POS acquires sticky primary accounts.

## Key Points — Project Phoenix / Platformization

- **[[Project Phoenix]]**: platformization program to enable global scale by 2026. Framed as "open-heart surgery" and "changing turbine engines at 45,000 feet."
- **Ravi** assigned as program manager.
- **Phase 1**: launch TSP (consolidates account transfers, cards, and other services). Team: Alex, Phil, Frank. Start Phoenix design system across POS and web.
- **Phase 2**: all teams adopt new design + native processes. Target June.
- **Phase 3**: beta new application; launch parallel backend using strangler pattern to replace legacy.
- **Definition of platformization**: one configurable product built by a global platform org, used by in-country business entities. Shift from separate Nigeria/UK builds.
- **Structure**: Nigeria becomes a business entity within the MFB; CBO role prepared for [[Toby]]. 4–5 business units (business banking, personal banking, digital banking, market research, operations, etc.). Business lines own strategy + customer response; platform centralizes product/engineering.
- **MoneyWalls migrating to platform**; business team operates MoneyWalls with a separate platform team.
- **Code freeze**: 2–3 month freeze expected during platformization with "keep the lights on" BAU preserved.

## Key Points — Kenya Launch

- **Confirmed** via acquisition of [[Sumac Microfinance Bank]] — three years of work to close.
- Sumac profile: ~60 people; top-5 Kenyan microfinance bank origin story (from savings-and-lending club).
- Primary acquisition rationale: **license** (license-led M&A model going forward).
- Recognition to [[Peter]] and [[Ross]] for their roles in the acquisition.
- Mr. John introduced as Sumac team leader.
- Sumac Board chair highlighted East Africa ~180M population (excl. South Sudan, Somalia) and commitment to support expansion.

## Key Points — Competitive / Regulatory Strategy

- **Verve pricing shock (Q4 2025)**: switched from flat pricing via regulatory loophole. Acquirers are capped and must accept all schemes, but schemes aren't price-regulated — creates rent-seeking leverage.
- **Action**: Dennis to negotiate Verve pricing down in exchange for committed volumes.
- **No rent-seeking posture** by Moniepoint — recognize [[Interswitch]] / Verve heritage but reject current tactics.
- **Price hikes not viable** — agent/merchant thin margins, price-sensitive; even small fee increases cause attrition to transfers/cash.
- **Regulatory strategy**: educating regulators on systemic risks, capital adequacy, foreclosure of demand, monopoly concerns. Central bank prioritizes systemic stability; [[FCCPC]] mandate constrained by CBN carve-outs. Pursuing legal reforms.
- **Predatory pricing watch**: 0% commercial bank offers currently ~0.3–0.4% share (from 0.2%). If a bank hits ~10% acquiring share, dominance/cross-subsidy concerns would apply.
- **Continue strategy despite scrutiny** — scrutiny near max expected; no retreat. Solve fundamentals, not regulatory arbitrage.
- **CRR burden**: ~45% CRR (45 kobo per 1 naira at CBN) — makes becoming a commercial bank unattractive despite licensing needs.

## Key Points — Product Priorities

- **MP35 POS end-of-life** requiring write-offs and replacement.
- **Real-time customer qualitative feedback systems** needed (CSAT pipelines into product teams).
- **Reliability and uptime as first-class metrics**.
- **VAS**: 27% MoM growth but far from potential. Improve UX + add rewards.
- **[[Moneybook]] (AI-native)**: current M1 retention reportedly 60% → 67%, still considered poor. Target 80% M1 leading to 60% M6 before aggressive CAC. If retained well, opens millions of non-POS small businesses (Instagram sellers, neighborhood shops) — potential defensible differentiator.
- **Key Account Management**: Pareto-aligned (10% of customers = 55% revenue) for white-glove service on large clients.
- **Full stack**: payments + credit + savings + insurance + software.
- **Pre-June deliverables**: VAS, account transfers ("Pay with MoneyPoint"), lending, POS device experience fixes, international launch, contactless, time-to-pay, middle-market lending readiness.

## Key Points — Market Expansion

- **Post-Kenya list**: South Africa, Morocco, Kenya, DRC (DRC flagged as Nigeria-like margin opportunity — "polarized, high-margin").
- **Smaller markets** can offer lower revenue but better margins + less competition.
- **Per-country cost falls** as platformization lowers incremental-market cost.
- **New hire** to develop "class strategy" for next-market selection — due upon June resumption.
- **Digital strategy** raises entry barriers via concentration (fewer higher-retention customers) vs. broad POS dispersion (acts like an "advert" inviting attacks).

## Key Decisions

- 20% PAT run-rate by end-2026; 30% by Q2 2026.
- Continue strategy despite regulatory scrutiny — solve fundamentals, no regulatory arbitrage.
- 2026 scope: platform migrations (MoneyWalls, MoneyPoint Nigeria), Kenya entry, 20% PAT.
- Nigeria vs. international contribution split defined **after** client strategy + platformization complete.
- License-led M&A for market entry (Sumac model).
