## Definition

Vertical integration means owning and controlling multiple stages of a product's creation and delivery rather than relying on third-party vendors at each layer. In technology, this typically means controlling the chip, the operating system, the application layer, and the services layer simultaneously.

## Why It Matters

The competitive advantage of vertical integration is not cost reduction — it's coherence. When one company controls the full stack, it can make design decisions that no multi-vendor ecosystem can replicate: hardware optimized for the OS, OS optimized for the application layer, applications that use hardware capabilities unavailable to third parties.

## Examples in Brain

- [[Apple]] — explicitly cites its "unique ability to design and develop its own operating systems, hardware, application software and services" as its core strategic differentiator (per [[Apple's_key_business_objectives]])

## Tradeoffs

- **Advantage**: superior integration, coherent user experience, control over product roadmap
- **Cost**: high capital and engineering investment to maintain competence across multiple domains; slower to adopt external innovations
